# Validated Case Patterns

These are portable lessons from prior development. They are not hard-coded examples and must not replace fresh survey understanding.

## Short Can Be Real

Some accepted respondents give very short answers that still name a real factor, choice, product attribute, or tradeoff. Shortness alone is not discard evidence.

## Polished Can Be Weak

Highly fluent answers can be suspicious when they are generic, ungrounded, repeated across respondents, or disconnected from the respondent’s closed answers.

## Speed Needs Coupling

Fast completion is most useful when it fails to stretch for harder tasks or when detailed text appears too quickly without grounding. Fast but coherent chains can be legitimate.

## Duplicate Technical Context Needs Semantics

Shared IP, device, supplier, or chain metadata matters most when supposedly independent rows also share response vectors, open-end templates, timing shapes, or incompatible identities.

## Client Rejection Is an Outcome, Not Proof

Client labels are valuable development data, but a rejected label does not automatically prove fraud. The system must learn the rejection boundary while keeping authenticity risk and client rejection probability separate.

## Accepted Rows Teach the Boundary

Accepted controls reveal what not to punish: concise human answers, rough language, legitimate extremes, and coherent low-effort but truthful behavior.

## Echo Benchmark Lessons

Echo exposed a conservative false-negative pattern. The reviewer often treated semantic invalidity as review-only because the respondent had a plausible brand or consumer chain. Future runs must treat invalid open ends, wrong-field answers, all-brand behavior, flat share allocation, and fully uniform ad grids as stronger when they converge, even when timing is normal or one part of the brand funnel looks coherent.

Normal or long timing is not a global defense. It can reflect interruption. It should not offset off-category text, all-brand selection, fully uniform ad matrices, or invalid other-specifies.

Accepted-row guardrails still matter. Do not exclude solely for speed, all-high ratings, misspellings, concise brand terms, or a one-brand story when the respondent answers the specific fields and the full chain is coherent.
